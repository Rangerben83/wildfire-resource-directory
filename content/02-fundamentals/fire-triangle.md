## Overview

Wildfire behavior is governed by the interaction of three primary factors, commonly known as the **fire behavior triangle**: fuels, weather, and topography. Understanding how these factors interact is essential for predicting fire spread, intensity, and ultimately for making effective management decisions.

::: {.callout-important}
## The Fire Behavior Triangle
The three essential factors that determine fire behavior:

1. **Fuels** - The combustible material (vegetation, structures)
2. **Weather** - Atmospheric conditions (wind, humidity, temperature)  
3. **Topography** - Terrain characteristics (slope, aspect, elevation)

These factors are interdependent - changing one affects how the others influence fire behavior.
:::

## Fire Behavior Overview

### Surface Fire Spread

Surface fires burn through ground-level fuels including grass, shrubs, forest litter, and fallen branches. Key characteristics:

- **Rate of spread (ROS)**: How quickly the fire front advances (chains/hour or m/s)
- **Flame length**: Height of flames above fuel bed (critical for suppression safety)
- **Fireline intensity**: Energy release rate per unit length of fire front (kW/m or Btu/ft/s)

Surface fires are generally more predictable and easier to model than crown fires, though they can still exhibit extreme behavior under the right conditions.

### Crown Fire Dynamics

Crown fires burn through the canopy of trees and represent some of the most dangerous wildfire conditions:

- **Passive crown fire**: Surface fire intermittently torches individual trees
- **Active crown fire**: Continuous fire spread through tree crowns, independent of surface fire
- **Crown fire initiation**: Requires sufficient surface fireline intensity to ignite canopy fuels
- **Crown fire propagation**: Sustained spread requires adequate canopy bulk density and favorable weather

The transition from surface to crown fire depends critically on:
- Canopy base height (vertical fuel continuity)
- Canopy bulk density (available crown fuel)
- Surface fire intensity (energy to initiate crowning)
- Wind speed (to sustain crown spread)

### Spotting and Ember Transport

Firebrands (burning embers) can be lofted by convective plumes and transported ahead of the main fire front, creating spot fires:

- **Generation**: Embers produced from burning vegetation and structures
- **Transport**: Carried by fire-generated winds and ambient atmospheric flows
- **Landing and ignition**: Depends on ember size, fuel moisture, and receptive fuel beds
- **Distance**: Can range from meters to several kilometers in extreme conditions

Spotting is a primary mechanism for rapid fire spread in the wildland-urban interface (WUI) and can overwhelm suppression efforts by creating multiple ignition points.

### Plume Rise and Smoke Generation

Fire-generated convection creates vertical plumes that:

- Transport smoke, ash, and embers
- Modify local wind patterns
- Can develop into pyrocumulonimbus clouds in extreme cases
- Affect regional air quality and visibility

## The Fire Behavior Triangle: Deep Dive

### 1. Fuels - The Primary Controllable Factor

Fuels are often the dominant factor in determining fire behavior and represent the primary variable that managers can modify through treatment.

#### Fuel Characteristics

**Fuel loading**: Mass of available fuel per unit area (tons/acre or kg/m²)
- Dead fuels: Classified by timelag (1-hr, 10-hr, 100-hr, 1000-hr)
- Live fuels: Herbaceous and woody vegetation

**Fuel moisture**: Water content as percentage of dry weight
- Critical threshold: typically 8-12% for active spread in dead fuels
- Live fuel moisture: varies seasonally, affects crown fire potential
- Fuel moisture conditioning drives day-to-day and seasonal fire activity

**Fuel arrangement**:
- Horizontal continuity: Spatial distribution across landscape
- Vertical structure: Layering from surface through canopy
- Fuel bed depth and packing ratio
- Canopy architecture: base height, bulk density, cover

**Fuel type**: Chemical and physical properties
- Conifer vs. hardwood
- Fine fuels (grasses) vs. heavy fuels (logs)
- Surface area-to-volume ratio (critical for ignition and spread)

#### Why Fuels Dominate in Planning

Studies consistently show that fuel characteristics are the primary driver of fire behavior in most planning contexts:

- Fuel treatments (thinning, prescribed fire) can reduce ROS by 50-80%
- Fuel moisture conditioning determines whether fire spreads at all
- Fuel spatial patterns control where fires can and cannot spread
- Unlike weather, fuels can be modified through management actions

### 2. Weather - The Dynamic Driver of Intensity

Weather provides the dynamic forcing that activates fuel potential and drives extreme fire behavior.

#### Critical Variables

**Wind speed and direction**:
- Primary driver of directional spread and fire front intensity
- Increases oxygen supply and flame tilt (radiation to unburned fuels)
- Creates spotting opportunities through enhanced convection
- Wind shifts can rapidly change fire behavior and threaten suppression forces

**Relative humidity (RH)**:
- Inversely related to fuel moisture (lower RH → drier fuels)
- Affects fuel conditioning over hours to days
- Critical thresholds: <25% RH often signals high fire danger
- Combined with temperature to determine vapor pressure deficit

**Temperature**:
- Affects fuel moisture through evapotranspiration
- Influences ignition probability and combustion efficiency
- High temperatures (>30°C/85°F) associated with extreme fire days

**Atmospheric stability**:
- Unstable conditions enhance vertical plume development
- Can lead to pyrocumulonimbus formation in extreme fires
- Affects smoke dispersion and air quality impacts

#### High-Percentile Weather in Planning

Fire planning typically uses 90th, 95th, or 97th percentile weather conditions:

- Represents "bad day" scenarios when fires are most difficult to control
- Accounts for extreme wind events (Santa Ana, foehn, downslope winds)
- Used in burn probability modeling and treatment prioritization
- IFTDSS "Auto97th" weather streams provide standardized extreme scenarios

### 3. Topography - The Multiplier

Topography modifies both fuels and weather effects, acting as a multiplier on fire behavior.

#### Slope Effects

**Rate of spread increases exponentially with upslope burning**:
- Slope preheats fuels ahead of the fire through radiation and convection
- Flames tilt closer to unburned fuels
- Doubling of ROS for every 10° increase in slope (Rothermel)
- Downslope fire spread is dramatically slower

**Critical slope thresholds**:
- >30% (17°): Significant increase in fire behavior
- >55% (30°): Extreme fire behavior possible even with moderate fuels/weather

#### Aspect and Solar Exposure

- South and west-facing slopes (Northern Hemisphere): 
  - Greater solar radiation → drier fuels → higher fire activity
  - Typically burned earlier in fire season
- North and east-facing slopes:
  - Moister fuels, denser vegetation
  - Often act as natural fire breaks

#### Terrain-Driven Winds

**Topographic channeling**:
- Canyons and valleys funnel and accelerate winds
- Can create extreme local wind conditions
- Particularly dangerous in steep, narrow drainages

**Slope and valley winds**:
- Upslope/upvalley during day (anabatic)
- Downslope/downvalley at night (katabatic)
- Can reinforce or oppose synoptic winds

**Foehn winds** (Chinook, Santa Ana, Diablo):
- Downslope wind events with rapid warming and drying
- Often associated with the most destructive wildfires
- Can persist for days, creating extended periods of extreme fire weather

#### Elevation Effects

- Temperature and humidity gradients with elevation
- Snowpack and moisture availability
- Vegetation transitions (fuel type changes)
- Lightning ignition patterns in mountainous terrain

## Interconnections: The Triangle in Action

The fire behavior triangle is not simply three independent factors - the interactions are critical:

### Fuels Set the Stage

Fuel characteristics establish the baseline potential for fire spread and intensity:
- Available energy (fuel loading)
- Ignitability (fuel moisture, surface area)
- Spatial continuity (fuel arrangement)

Without adequate fuels, weather and topography cannot drive significant fire behavior.

### Weather Ignites and Accelerates

Weather activates fuel potential and determines when fires spread aggressively:
- Wind increases ROS and spotting
- Low humidity dries fuels to critical moisture levels
- High temperatures extend burning period and enhance ignition

The same fuels can exhibit vastly different behavior under different weather conditions.

### Topography Multiplies Effects

Terrain amplifies or moderates the fuels-weather interaction:
- Slope increases effective heating of fuels
- Aspect determines fuel moisture distribution
- Terrain channels and accelerates winds
- Elevation creates fuel and weather gradients

Steep slopes can turn moderate fire behavior into extreme runs even without extreme weather.

### Example: The Perfect Storm

Consider a scenario where all three factors align for extreme fire:

**Fuels**: Drought-stressed conifer forest, high canopy bulk density, continuous fuels
**Weather**: Santa Ana wind event (40+ mph), 10% RH, 35°C, unstable atmosphere  
**Topography**: Steep (40%) south-facing slope, narrow canyon

Result: Active crown fire with 100+ m flame lengths, spotting 2+ km ahead, overwhelming suppression capabilities. This combination drove many catastrophic California fires.

## WUI and Structure Fire Dynamics

The fire behavior triangle also applies at the wildland-urban interface (WUI), with additional complexity from structures and human-modified fuels.

### Parcel-Scale Fire Spread

At the WUI scale, fire behavior is determined by:

**Parcel fuels**:
- Ornamental vegetation (often non-native and highly flammable)
- Defensible space (or lack thereof)
- Surface fuels: mulch, wood decks, stored materials
- Vertical fuel continuity: shrubs to trees to structures

**Local topography**:
- Slope immediately around structures
- Aspect relative to prevailing winds
- Topographic position (ridgetop, mid-slope, valley)

**Micro-weather**:
- Building-scale wind patterns
- Heat reflection and radiation from structures
- Local humidity gradients

### Structure Ignition Pathways

Buildings in the WUI are threatened through three mechanisms:

1. **Flame contact**: Direct impingement from vegetation or adjacent structures
2. **Thermal radiation**: Radiant heat from flames (can ignite at 6+ meters)
3. **Ember/firebrand ignition**: Most common WUI structure loss mechanism

### NIST WUI Hazard Scale

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) developed a WUI hazard assessment framework based on:

**F - Fuels**: Vegetation type, density, proximity to structure  
**T - Topography**: Slope, aspect, position  
**L - Local conditions**: Building materials, defensible space  
**W - Weather**: Wind exposure, fire weather conditions

This FTLW framework quantifies WUI exposure and guides mitigation priorities.

### Importance in Risk Assessment

WUI fire dynamics determine:
- Structure loss probability
- Community evacuation requirements
- Insurance exposure and premium
- Mitigation effectiveness (defensible space, hardening)

## Risk to Society and Systems

Wildfire impacts extend far beyond the burned area, affecting multiple societal systems.

### Direct Impacts

**Structure loss**: 
- Residential and commercial buildings
- Critical infrastructure (power, water, communications)
- Historical and cultural resources

**Life safety**:
- Civilian casualties (typically during evacuation)
- Firefighter injuries and fatalities
- Public health (smoke exposure, psychological trauma)

**Ecosystem damage**:
- High-severity fire effects on sensitive ecosystems
- Soil sterilization and long-term productivity loss
- Wildlife habitat destruction

### Cascading Impacts

**Utility disruptions**:
- Power outages (PSPS, fire damage to transmission/distribution)
- Water system impacts (treatment plant damage, contamination)
- Communications infrastructure failure

**Business interruption**:
- Direct damage to commercial facilities
- Supply chain disruptions
- Tourism and recreation impacts
- Agricultural losses

**Healthcare disruption**:
- Hospital evacuations
- Smoke-related respiratory emergencies
- Mental health impacts
- Overwhelmed emergency medical services

### Long-Term Consequences

**Smoke and air quality**:
- Regional smoke transport (hundreds to thousands of km)
- PM2.5 and toxic compound exposure
- Multi-week to multi-month air quality degradation
- Public health burden (respiratory, cardiovascular)

**Watershed degradation**:
- Loss of forest cover increases runoff and erosion
- Sediment loading to streams and reservoirs
- Water quality impacts (turbidity, nutrients, contaminants)
- Debris flow risk in steep terrain

**Post-fire flooding and erosion**:
- Hydrophobic soils increase runoff
- Loss of vegetation reduces infiltration
- Increased peak flows and flood risk
- Reservoir sedimentation

**Economic and social**:
- Property value impacts
- Insurance market disruption
- Community displacement and trauma
- Recovery costs (often billions of dollars)

## Implications for Modeling

Understanding the fire behavior triangle is essential for effective use of wildfire models:

**Model selection**: Choose models appropriate for the dominant factors in your application
- Fuel-dominated → Landscape models with detailed fuel inputs
- Weather-dominated → Coupled atmosphere-fire models
- WUI → Physics-based CFD for structure exposure

**Input prioritization**: Focus data acquisition on the factors that matter most
- Planning: High-quality fuel data and extreme weather scenarios
- Operations: Real-time weather and dynamic fuel moisture
- WUI: Fine-scale fuels, parcel geometry, building characteristics

**Uncertainty quantification**: Recognize which factors drive output variance
- Weather uncertainty often dominates short-term forecasts
- Fuel uncertainty matters more for long-term risk assessment
- Topography is generally well-known but can have local surprises

**Scenario design**: Explore the interaction space
- Test sensitivity to each factor
- Examine coupled effects (fuel treatment + weather + slope)
- Identify thresholds where behavior changes dramatically
